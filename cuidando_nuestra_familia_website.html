Latino cultural values are not barriers to accessing care — they are profound sources of resilience, community, and healing. Research consistently shows that families who draw on these values navigate disability with greater strength. This section explores six core values and how to apply them in disability and caregiving contexts.

How values interact with the system
Strength
Familismo as a care network

Research by Magaña & Vanegas (2021) shows that Latino families of children with intellectual and developmental disabilities often rely on extensive kin networks for daily care. This informal support system reduces institutional dependence and provides emotional continuity — something the formal care system often cannot replicate. The key is recognizing this as a resource to build on, not a reason to provide less formal support.

Tension to navigate
Marianismo and caregiver burden

The ideal of marianismo — in which women are selfless, nurturing, and sacrificing — means Latina caregivers often put their own health last. Studies by Mendez-Luck & Anthony (2016) found that U.S.-born and immigrant Mexican women differ in how strongly they internalize these beliefs, but both groups face elevated caregiver stress. Understanding this tension is the first step to finding balance without abandoning cultural identity.

Spiritual dimension
Espiritualidad in autism families

Salkas et al. (2016) found that spirituality plays a significant protective role for Latino families of children with autism spectrum disorder. Families reported that faith gave meaning to their caregiving experience, helped them cope with uncertainty, and connected them to community. Providers who ignore or dismiss spiritual frameworks miss a powerful tool in family-centered care.

Research insight
Acculturation and identity

Velcoff, Hernandez & Keys (2010) found that Latinos with disabilities who are more acculturated tend to access vocational rehabilitation differently than those less acculturated — neither pattern being superior, but both being shaped by the tension between the U.S. disability system's individualistic model and Latino collectivist values. Caminero-Santangelo (2012) further explores how Latinidad itself is a constructed, contested identity that varies by nationality, generation, and experience.

Key takeaway: Cultural values are not static. They evolve across generations and immigration experiences. Effective support respects this complexity rather than applying a monolithic "Latino culture" lens. What helps one family may not apply to another.

Special education and the IEP process
Research finding

Mortier & Arias (2023) documented how Latina mothers navigate special education, finding that the system often assumes families understand concepts like "IEP," "due process," and "least restrictive environment" — when in fact these are deeply technical and culturally unfamiliar concepts. Language barriers compound the problem: parents who don't speak English fluently are often handed translated documents without explanation.

Rios & Burke (2021) reviewed the literature and identified key facilitators of positive special education experiences: having a bilingual advocate, building personal relationships with teachers (personalismo), and connecting with other Latino parents who have navigated the system. Barriers included transportation, work schedule conflicts, mistrust of government institutions, and lack of culturally adapted materials.

Know this: Under IDEA, schools must provide evaluation, IEP documents, and meeting notices in your home language at no cost to you. If this is not happening, you have the right to request it formally and to bring a bilingual advocate to any meeting.

The caregiver experience in Latino families
Alzheimer's & dementia
Caring for a parent with dementia

Jaldin et al. (2023) studied Latino family caregivers of individuals with Alzheimer's and related dementias, finding that familismo strongly shaped how families understood the caregiver role — viewing it as a moral obligation and source of meaning rather than a burden. However, this framing also made it harder for caregivers to set limits or accept outside help. The study highlights the need for culturally tailored support that honors this commitment while offering practical relief.

Fathers & disability stigma
Fathers with a child with a disability

Mogro-Wilson et al. (2024) found that Latino cultural concepts of fatherhood — machismo, being the provider and protector — significantly predicted stigma levels among fathers of children with disabilities. Fathers who felt their child's disability "reflected on them" reported higher stigma and lower engagement with services. Programs that directly address Latino fatherhood identity, rather than treating all parents the same, lead to better family outcomes.

Liberation and advocacy in practice

Balcazar et al. (2012) introduced a "liberation" framework for understanding how Latino immigrant families of children with disabilities move from passivity to active advocacy. The process has three phases: (1) critical consciousness — recognizing that your child has rights and that systems can and must respond; (2) skill building — learning how to communicate with schools, providers, and government agencies; and (3) social action — joining or forming networks that push for systemic change.

This framework is not abstract — it has been applied in community settings across Chicago and beyond. Organizations like Grupo Salto explicitly use capacity-building approaches (Balcazar, Keys & Suárez-Balcázar, 2021) to help Latinos with disabilities develop self-determination, navigate independent living, and advocate for policy change at the local level.

What the research tells us

Suárez-Balcázar et al. (2025) developed a conceptual model for co-creating culturally tailored interventions with Latina immigrant caregivers of children with disabilities. The model centers community participation — caregivers are not just recipients of programs but co-designers. This participatory approach leads to higher engagement, better outcomes, and more sustainable programs.

Montenegro et al. (2022) surveyed caregivers of autistic children across Latin America and found that stigma and service barriers are not unique to the U.S. — they are pervasive across Argentina, Colombia, Ecuador, and Peru. Families reported that perceived stigma was the single strongest predictor of delayed help-seeking, more so than income or geographic access to services.
